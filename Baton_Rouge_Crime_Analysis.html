Baton Rouge Crime Analysis¶

Project Overview¶

This project analyzes reported crime incidents in Baton Rouge, focusing on violent crime trends by neighborhood and over time.

Questions¶

  1. Which neighborhoods have the highest violent crime burden?
  2. How did violent crime rates change from 2020–2025?
  3. Did the decline in violent crime rate reflect fewer violent crimes, or a shift toward property/society crimes?

def categorize(value):
    
    
    if pd.isna(value):
        return "OTHER"
    
   
    elif "PERSON" in value:
        return "PERSON"
    
    
    elif "PROPERTY" in value:
        return "PROPERTY"
    
    elif "SOCIETY" in value:
        return "SOCIETY"

    else:
        return "OTHER"

crime['CRIME AGAINST CLEAN'] = crime['CRIME AGAINST'].apply(categorize)

Redundant columns containing overlapping information were consolidated to simplify the dataset and improve consistency. All columns related to statute information were merged into a single STATUTE column, while NIBRS DESCRIPTION and NIBRS CODE were combined into a unified NIBRS column. The original fragmented columns were then removed to reduce duplication and ensure each variable was represented clearly.

The CRIME AGAINST column contained inconsistent and overlapping labels. To standardize this variable, values were grouped into four categories: PERSON, PROPERTY, SOCIETY, and OTHER. This transformation allows for more consistent comparison of crime types across neighborhoods and over time. The original column was replaced with the cleaned and standardized version.

Additional cleaning was performed to ensure that the dataset only includes incidents reported within Baton Rouge, Louisiana.

The original dataset does not explicitly distinguish between violent and non-violent offenses. To enable focused analysis, a separate dataframe was created containing only crimes classified as violent.

For this project, violent crime is defined as offenses involving direct harm or threat to another person. Both STATUTE and NIBRS classifications were used to identify relevant offenses, including assault, homicide, robbery, and related categories.

Certain categories were excluded from this definition, such as traffic, juvenile, and nuisance-related offenses, as they do not directly involve harm to another individual. This filtering ensures that the resulting dataset aligns with the working definition of violent crime used in this analysis.

Broad or non-specific categories such as “ALL OTHER OFFENSES” were excluded to ensure the violent crime dataset reflects clearly defined offense types.Value counts within the violent dataframe were checked to ensure only crimes which fall within our working definition are included.

Violent crime patterns were analyzed across neighborhoods to identify areas with both high crime volume and a high proportion of violent incidents.

Total crimes and violent crimes were calculated for each neighborhood, and a proportion of violent crime was derived by comparing the two. To ensure meaningful comparisons, neighborhoods with very low total crime counts were excluded.

A combined metric was then created to highlight neighborhoods with both high crime volume and a high concentration of violent offenses, allowing for more balanced comparison across areas.

Visualizations and Interpretation¶

The neighborhood with the highest combined burden of violent crime, considering both total volume and proportion, is North Baton Rouge. A line chart was created to visualize the proportion of violent crime over time within this neighborhood.

The neighborhood with the second highest combined score is Brookstown. A second line representing violent crime rates in Brookstown was plotted alongside North Baton Rouge to allow for direct comparison of trends over time. This comparison helps determine whether observed patterns are specific to a single neighborhood or reflective of broader trends.

Crime composition within North Baton Rouge was compared to that of Brookstown was analyzed. Crimes are categorized into three types: violent crime, crimes against property, and crimes against society. This provides insight into how the distribution of crime types differs across neighborhoods.

The chart for North Baton Rouge (2020–2025) shows the proportion of violent crimes relative to total reported incidents. A clear decline in violent crime proportion is observed between 2020 and 2022, followed by a period of relative stability with a slight increase in later years.

However, caution should be taken when interpreting earlier years in the dataset. Lower data volume or incomplete reporting may make early values less representative of true crime patterns. If early data disproportionately reflects violent incidents, the observed decline may be partially influenced by changes in reporting rather than actual decreases in violent crime.

Additionally, external factors such as the COVID-19 pandemic may have influenced both crime patterns and reporting practices during this period. Changes in policing, public behavior, or data collection methods could contribute to the trends observed, particularly during the years of most significant change.

Conclusion¶

Using publicly available crime data, crime trends in Baton Rouge were analyzed. Crimes categorized as violent were compiled into a separate dataframe to compare violent crime rates across neighborhoods. Patterns in reported crime were explored with the goal of better understanding neighborhood-level violent crime risk within Baton Rouge.

The majority of reported crimes across neighborhoods were crimes against property. Although violent crime proportions varied somewhat by neighborhood, they generally remained within a similar range. Overall, North Baton Rouge demonstrated the highest total crime burden. When compared to Brookstown, which carried the second-highest proportion of violent crime, both neighborhoods followed very similar trends over time. This suggests that crime patterns within Baton Rouge are likely influenced more by broader city-wide factors than by isolated neighborhood-specific events.

Additional analysis would be valuable to further confirm and expand upon the trends identified in this project. Geographic mapping could provide deeper insight into spatial crime distribution across neighborhoods. Future analysis could also incorporate predictive modeling techniques to better understand crime trends and potentially support future policy or resource allocation decisions.

Overall, this project demonstrates how public crime data can be cleaned, analyzed, and interpreted to better understand neighborhood-level crime patterns and support future data-driven research.

Limitations¶

The dataset used in this project was publicly sourced, meaning the consistency and completeness of the data could not be fully verified. Additionally, reported crimes are not representative of all crimes committed, as many incidents may go unreported. The classification of “violent” crimes also required interpretation and categorization decisions during the data-cleaning process, which may influence results.
